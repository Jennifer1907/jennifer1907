## What I Got Right

### 1. I learned to communicate before I learned to model

### 2. I picked depth over breadth in my second year

The temptation as a new analyst is to learn everything: SQL, Python, R, Tableau, Power BI, Spark, Looker, dbt...

I got much more value from going deep on Python + SQL + one BI tool than from being mediocre at everything. When you're the team's Python expert, people come to you. When you're "okay at everything," people go to the specialist.

### 3. I got close to a domain

### 4. I built in public (sort of)

Writing internal write-ups of my analyses, sharing findings in Slack, volunteering to present at data team all-handsâ€”all of these compounded my visibility over time. People outside my immediate team started tagging me when they had data questions.

## What I Wasted Time On

### Chasing certifications before building projects

I spent three months of evenings on a cloud certification that nobody has ever asked me about in an interview. The project I built the month after thatâ€”a churn model for a nonprofit using Kaggle dataâ€”came up in every subsequent conversation.

Build things. Then document them. The certification is the resume line; the project is the interview.

### Perfecting analyses nobody read

I once spent two weeks refining a segmentation model. By the time I was done, the product team had already made the decision it was supposed to inform.

Ship an 80% analysis on time, then iterate. A good-enough answer today beats a perfect answer next month.

### Optimising for title vs learning

I turned down a senior analyst role at a small company to stay at Shopify as an analyst II, because I knew I'd learn more from the Shopify infrastructure and team. Two years later I got promoted to senior and then lateral-hired at Stripe at a higher level than the role I'd turned down.

Optimise for slope, not intercept.

## Advice I'd Give My 2019 Self

- Find a senior analyst whose work you admire and ask to review your work with them monthly. Most people will say yes.
- Write up your analyses even when nobody asks. The habit of documenting decisions is worth its weight in gold.
- Develop a point of view. Analysts who only report what the data says are replaceable. Analysts who say "here's what I think we should do, and here's why" become indispensable.
- Your first company matters less than the people you learn from there.
